Web Accessibility Checklist

Your go-to accessibility design toolkit.

Why should you, as a designer, make accessibility part of your design process?

Did you know that around 12.7% of the US population and 16% of the global population identifies with having a disability? That's no small number! So, as a designer, why not make accessibility an integral part of your design process? Not only does it open up your creations to a wider audience, but it also enhances the overall user experience for everyone.

Think about it: when you create accessible designs, you're actually making the web a better place for everyone. Accessibility improves usability, making websites more convenient and enjoyable to use, regardless of individual abilities. "But wait," you might wonder, "Won't designing for accessibility limit my creativity?" Not at all! In fact, it's quite the opposite. Accessibility guidelines and best practices act as helpful tools rather than stifling constraints. They encourage you to find innovative solutions and think outside the box. Picture it as a colorful palette of possibilities, allowing you to create enchanting experiences that captivate users from all walks of life.

"Wouldn't it costme a lot of time and effort to create accessible designs", you might also ask. Quite the opposite! Integrating accessibility into your design process doesn't have to cost you extra time. By considering accessibility from the start, you naturally weave it into your design framework. It becomes an organic part of your creative journey, rather than a separate and time-consuming task. And that's why I am introducing handing this respource to you; hoping that I can make it easier for you to incorporate accessibility in your design process. Think of this as your trusty companion, accompanying you throughout the design process, and making sure you design useful, usable, and accessible interfaces.

Now that we have established that it’s important to think about accessibility…

Let's dive deeper into the different experiences that users have on the web, and what creating an accessible experience means. Accessibility is not just about providing alternative text for images; it encompasses a multitude of considerations that ensure a truly inclusive web experience for everyone.

When it comes to navigating the web, various accessibility issues come into play, catering to different needs and abilities. Let's explore some of the key dimensions:

  • Cognitive Accessibility: Designing with cognitive accessibility in mind involves organizing content logically, using clear language, and providing helpful cues and signposts along the way.
  • Visual Accessibility: Incorporating proper color contrast, descriptive image captions, scalable fonts, and adaptable layouts to ensure that your design resonates with everyone, no matter their visual abilities.
  • Auditory Accessibility: Catering to individuals who are deaf or hard of hearing. Including closed captions, transcripts, and alternative audio descriptions for multimedia elements lets every user embrace the full spectrum of your content.
  • Motor Accessibility: Addressing the needs of those with physical disabilities, ensuring they can navigate and interact with web interfaces comfortably. Designing with motor accessibility in mind involves providing keyboard navigation options, allowing sufficient time for interactions, and avoiding elements that rely solely on precise mouse movements.
  • Speech Accessibility: Accommodating individuals with speech impairments, allowing them to engage with the web through alternative input methods.

As a designer, it's important to center your designs around your user needs, weaving together different cognitive, visual, auditory, motor, and speech elements. Before we jump into what you should do, it's important to understand how different users can experience the same website in a very different way. For example, I used a tool called Toptal to see how users with achromatopsia (a type of color blindness) see Amazon's website.

See how drastically different it can be? This is why guidleines like the Web Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) exist to enable developers and designers to create a just and accessible experience for all. I compiled all those different guidlines into a list that you, my fellow designer, can use to guide your deisgn process. Excited to see the great designs you create!

Accessible Design Checklist

It's important to organize the information on the page in a way that will make it easy for users to naviagte and understand, esepcially users who utilize screen readers. Here is a video illustrating how it's like to navigate a webpage with a screen reader:

    • Screen readers usually start by reading the page's headings, starting from the highest-level heading and moving down to lower-level headings. Those different sections or "roles" are specified in code using what are called tags. For example, the headings that would be read first will have the following tag: <h1> and the second one will have: <h2>... and it goes on till 6. This becomes relevant for you as a designer when communicating your designs to developers

    Headings provide an outline of the content structure. Next, screen readers typically read the links on the page.

    Screen readers then move on to reading form controls, such as input fields, checkboxes, radio buttons, and dropdown menus. After going through the structural elements, screen readers read the main content of the page, including paragraphs, sentences, and individual text elements.

    • In terms of code, developers can distinguish this hierarchy of information using what is called a tag:

    For headings: <h1>, <h2>, ... up to <h6>

    For paragraphs: <p>

    It's important to clearly communicate the roles that different bodies of text take on the page to ensure an accessible implementation. You can read more about Information Hierarchy here.

    • Readable fonts are easy to read, while legible fonts are easy to distinguish from each other. Some popular accessible fonts are Tahoma, Calibri, Helvetica, Arial, Verdana, and SF.

    None of these seem to fit your design/brand? You can learn more about what makes a font accessible here.

    • The rule of thumb is to use at least an effective font size of 16px, but this might vary based on the typeface. No text should be below 12px.

    Protip: for a responsive web experience, your font size should be in relative terms (e.g. rem) instead of pixels. This makes the font sioze scale relative to the screen size.

    16px = 1rem

    You can read more about this here. Have all your font sizes in pixels already, use this conversion tool.

    • Another good rule of thumb: set the line height to be at approximately 150% of the font size.

    • Providing alt text enables users with visual impairment to be able to perceieve and enagage with the visual content on your site. The text alternative should be at least a short description conveying the essential information presented by the image
    Once provided, the alt text can be associated with the image using the "alt" property. Here is an example:

    <img src="image.jpg" alt="description of image">

    • Instead, use "real" text and use CSS to visually style it to achieve dynamic visual designs.

    • For complex figures like graphs, captions are important to provide the context needed to understand the content. Here is an example of how this can be translated to code:

    <figure>
    <img src="image.jpg" alt="description of image">
    <figcaption>
    Caption describing the image
    </figcaption>
    </figure>

    Protip: Want to learn more about image accessibility? Check out WCAG's image tutorial for a more in-depth guide.

    • It's recommended a target size of at least 44 x 44 pixels to ensure that it's easy to interact with, especially for users with physical disabilities.

    Protip: You can test the sizes of your buttons across different multiple devices on Figma and Sketch using Adee

    • How is the button going to behave when the user hovers over it? How is it going to behave when it's disabled?
    It's important to think about how to indicate those states to show the users when they are interacting with the button. Those states can be translated to code through properties like :focus, :hover, :active

    Protip: Check out this article on styling for different states

    • Modals/popups should be concise to make it easy for the users to digest and manipulate information.

    • Users should be able to dismiss the modal through some sort of CTA, (Close/x), or by tapping away from the modal.

    Protip: Check out this article on accessible modals

Here is an example of how to add an in-text link:

<a href="https://www.google.com">Google</a>

The "a" tag (wait, what is a tag?) is used for creating links, just like what the heading tags we discussed earlier are used to create different heading effects. The actual link goes into the "href" attribute of the tag, while the text you want to be displayed is between the two tag arrows.

The code above renders on the screen as following:

Google

    • You want to properly set the user's expectations of where this link is going to take them by using meaningful and concise text, just like we used "Google" in the example.

    • The default browser settings makes links underlined and in a blue color. Those properties are adjustable through code, but make sure that you specify how you want to indicate links in your hand-off to developers.

    • What happens when I click the link? Does it open in a new tab or in the same tab? Does it open a modal? Those are properties that can be specified in code. Make sure you think about this interaction and specify it in your design.
